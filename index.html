Hoover Bridge


Trail Info

The City of Hoover has installed a pedestrian bridge over the Cahaba River at the Old Rocky Ridge ballfields. This provides access to the undeveloped land across the river from the ballfields that have a number of unimproved trials through the Cahaba River floodplain. The view from the bridge is nice and the trails offer a chance to catch a glimpse of the local wildlife. These bottomlands are dominated by Japanese privet, but there are some big, old hardwoods as well.

History

Tannehill Ironworks

Tannehill Ironworks Historical State Park has more than 1,500 acres in three counties set aside for hiking, camping and outdoor recreation. A miniature railroad chugs through the pines. From spring through fall, the blacksmith, miller and craftsmen demonstrate their trades. Craft shops occupy restored pioneer cabins and artisans chat with visitors from their front porches. Steeped in history, Tannehill feels timeless. The cotton gin, pioneer farm and working gristmill preserve a long-gone way of life. Hiking trails retrace historic roadways. Artifacts of Alabama's 19th century iron industry displayed in the Iron and Steel Museum put in perspective the massive stone furnaces, Tannehill's awe-inspiring centerpiece.

West Blocton Coke Ovens

Originally opened in 1996, the West Blocton Coke Ovens Park is situated among the ruins of 467 ovens that were capable of producing 600 tons of coke daily. Construction on the ovens began in 1887 and the first coke was produced in 1888. The Blocton ovens complex was once one of the largest of its kind in the state. Today the park features walking trails, picnic tables, a boardwalk and plenty of plants, trees and flowers. The natural beauty provides opportunities to exercise or relax while the historic beehive ovens provide an opportunity to learn about a key component in Alabama's industrial history.

Brierfield Ironworks

Brierfield Ironworks Historical State Park is a 45-acre park in Bibb County located between Centreville and Montevallo. It preserves the site of an important Civil War-era ironworks and rolling mill. The park was established in 1976 by the Bibb County Commission at the urging of the Bibb County Heritage Association.

Old Cawhaba

Cahawba was once Alabama's state capital (1820-1826) and a thriving antebellum river town. It became a ghost town shortly after the Civil War. Today it is an important archaeological site and a place of picturesque ruins. Nature has reclaimed much of Old Cahawba, but historians and archaeologists from the Alabama Historical Commission are working hard to uncover Cahawba's historic past and to create a full time interpretive park.

Moundville

The Moundville site, occupied from around A.D. 1000 until A.D. 1450, is a large settlement of Mississippian culture on the Black Warrior River in central Alabama. At the time of Moundville's heaviest residential population, the community took the form of a three hundred-acre village built on a bluff overlooking the river.

Tates Take Out

Tates Take Out

  Private, unimproved, but easily accessible.  This property belongs to the Presbytery of Sheppards and Lapsley. From Tate"s Take-out to the Cahaba National Wildlife Refuge is 8.5 river miles or about a 5 hour paddle, if the water is good.

There are quite a few rocky shoals in this section, slowing progress when the Centreville gage is less than 1000 cfs.  Shorter floats from Booth"s Ford (about 7.5 river miles) or from the Presbytery 1 put-in (4 river miles) would take less time.

Lily Trip Beach

Lily Trip Beach

Public, unimproved, but a good put-in/take-out. Open dawn to dusk. Parking is more available just upstream. From here, you can paddle downstream and then paddle upstream back to your starting point. This allows great flexibility in how much total time you spend paddling.

Caffee Creek

Caffee Creek

Public, unimproved, but accessible. Parking is very limited. From the formal put-in at the upstream end of the refuge to Caffee Creek is 2 river miles or about an hours paddle. You can also do a "round-trip" from here if the Centreville gage shows the flow is 400 cfs or less.

Pratts Ferry Bridge

Pratts Ferry Bridge

Private, open to the public. This land belongs to The Nature Conservancy. From the Cahaba River National Wildlife Refuge to Pratt"s Ferry is 7.5 river miles and a 3.5 or 4 hour float if the Centreville gage reads 600 cfs or more. Less than that will result in a lot of dragging over rocky shoals. Less than 400 cfs makes this no fun.

Harrisburg Bridge

Harrisburg Bridge

Public, unimproved access at a county bridge. There is a dirt path off the dirt road on the east side of the river and the south side of the bridge. The access is steep and muddy, but not very far from parking. From Centreville to Harrisburg is 11.7 river miles or about 6 hours paddling. Harrisburg to Heiberger is 11.3 river miles, again, about 6 hours paddling time.

Perry Lake Park

Perry Lake Park

Public park open dawn to dusk, but the gate is locked up after sunset. This is a flatwater ramble through the cypress and tupelos. You can paddle for several hours exploring the three interconnected arms of this oxbow complex.

Radford Access

Radford Access

Sprott to Radford is about 7.5 miles or about 4 hours of paddling. This is one of the shortest floats in the coastal plain portion of the Cahaba. The old iron bridge has collapsed into the river just below the access, so exercise caution to avoid being caught up on the steel "strainer" the wreckage of the bridge creates.

Highway 80 - Marion Junction

Public, unimproved access under the US 80 bridge. There is a long and very steep sandy drop to the river. The Suttle to US 80 segment is about 9.7 river miles or about 5 hours of floating. US 80 to Hwy 22 is over 14 miles (almost 7 hours of continuous paddling).

Alabama River Take Out
